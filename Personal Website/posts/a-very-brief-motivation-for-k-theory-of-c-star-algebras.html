A Very Brief Motivation for $K$-Theory of $C^*$-algebras

Download PDF

In my time as a student, I have never ceased to be amazed by the wit needed to blaze a new path through math. Fourier analysis, Galois theory, functional analysis — genius ideas brewed in the minds of the greatest mathematicians to live. One may think they could never dream up such ideas, yet on the first day of classes, a wonderful exposé of motivation illuminates how one could make such a discovery. Having lived with such motivations all my life, I picked up my first textbook on $K$-theory of $C^*$-algebras, and like a kid in their first literature class, I could only think: “who, what, why, how?” Alas, there is no kid here, so we venture on in discovering the motivation for operator $K$-theory.

To unpathed waters, undreamed shores — William Shakespeare

The origins of $K$-theory begin in the breeding grounds of algebraic geometry (as many ideas seem to nowadays). Beginning with a complex projective algebraic manifold $X$, Grothendieck was able to define two Abelian groups, which encoded information about algebraic objects defined over $X$. Of these two groups, we are most concerned with $K^0(X)$ — a group defined by the vector bundles over $X$. Collecting the motivation for $K^0(X)$ and Bott’s periodicity theorem, Atiyah and Hirzebruch birthed topological $K$-theory [1].

To illustrate how topological $K$-theory came to be, we parallel Grothendieck’s works for a topological space $Y$. Let $\text{Vect}(Y)$ denote the set of isomorphism classes of vector bundles over $Y$. One can show that with direct sums of vector bundles as an operation, $(\text{Vect}(Y), \oplus)$ is an Abelian semigroup. Pursuant of an Abelian group, we can force artificial inverses into $\text{Vect}(Y)$ by applying the Grothendieck construction. Thus, we are left with an Abelian group $K(Y)$, and in fact, a commutative ring when equipped with tensor products of vector bundles. With our $K$-group defined, it can be established that $K(\cdot)$ is a contravariant functor from the category of topological vector spaces $\textbf{Top}$ to the category of commutative rings $\textbf{CRing}$, and the rest of topological $K$-theory follows [2].

Motivated by topological $K$-theory, we attempt to construct a $K$-theory for $C^*$-algebras. Such an analog already seems natural to us, as in the case of a unital commutative $C^*$-algebra $A$, we already know that the Gelfand-Naimark theorem identifies $A$ with $C(X)$ for some compact Hausdorff space $X$, and hence we can apply the topological $K$-theory from above. We begin with the case in which $A$ is unital. A natural first step is to define our vector bundles over $A$, but in trying to do so, we immediately run into an issue — $A$ is not commutative. At first glance, this doesn’t seem problematic, but when you dive into the details, there’s no longer a nice notion of the local trivialization of a vector bundle. To skim over the hard details, one can see where difficulties arise in trying to define a homeomorphism between the neighbourhood of a point in $A$ and the pre-image of the neighbourhood under the bundle projection. We thus need to generalize our notion of vector bundle in a manner that doesn’t require a notion of geometry for $A$.

Just when it all seems to come crumbling down, we find ourselves a cop out. Let’s take a look at this bundle of bandaids we have found for our theory:

Theorem. The Serre-Swan theorem

Let $X$ be a compact Hausdorff space, and $C(X)$ it’s ring of continuous complex-valued functions. Then, the category of complex finite-rank vector bundles over $X$ is equivalent to the category of finitely generated projective modules over $C(X)$.

It follows that in simple cases, we can view the study of vector bundles over $X$ as equivalent to the study of finitely generated projective modules over $C(X)$. With $C(X)$ being isomorphic to a unital commutative $C^*$-algebra, we can then try to study finitely generated projective modules over arbitrary unital $C^*$-algebras. Indeed, for a unital $C^*$-algebra $A$, recall that we define $P_\infty(A) = \bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \{p \in M_n(A): p \ \text{is a projection}\}$. We further say $p \sim q$ if there exists a (not necessarily square) matrix $v$ such that $p = v v^*$ and $q = v^* v$. The set $P_\infty(A)/\sim$ with operation $p \oplus q = \text{diag}(p, q)$ is isomorphic to the set of finitely generated projective modules over $A$, and considering the quotient under stable equivalence followed by the Grothendieck construction, we recover an abelian group $K_0(A)$, from which the $K$-theory of $C^*$-algebras follows [3].

Ultimately, the motivation for operator $K$-theory is the bridge provided by the Serre-Swan theorem. Defining $K$-theory via the isomorphism classes of vector bundles inherently relies on the geometry of the topological base space. By bridging from a geometric theory to a purely algebraic theory defined via finitely generated projective modules, we can then define our $K$-theory for $C^*$-algebras.
